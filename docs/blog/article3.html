Is It Still Worth Going to School for Advertising?

By Evans Mathibe • February 2026

This is perhaps the most common question I get from young South Africans looking to enter the advertising industry. With AI tools becoming increasingly sophisticated, and with many agencies now hiring based on portfolio rather than qualifications, the traditional path to advertising careers has been disrupted.

The Traditional Path vs. The New Reality

Ten years ago, the conventional wisdom was clear: get a degree in advertising or marketing, intern at an agency, and work your way up. Today, that path is no longer the only option—and in some cases, it's not even the best one.

I've hired dozens of creatives over the years. What I look for isn't a diploma—it's the ability to think creatively, to solve problems, and to produce work that connects with audiences. These skills can be developed in many ways, formal education being just one of them.

What University Actually Offers

Let me be clear: a formal education in advertising isn't worthless. Universities teach foundational concepts—brand strategy, consumer behavior, market research, media planning—that take years to learn on the job.

What they often don't teach well is the practical skill of actually making advertising. Brief writing, concept development, presentation skills—these are best learned through practice, not theory.

The real question isn't whether school is worth it—it's what you're going to do with what you learn there.

The Alternative Paths

I've seen incredible talent emerge from YouTube tutorials, online courses, and pure self-directed learning. Tools like ChatGPT, Midjourney, and Canva have democratized creative production in ways that were unimaginable a decade ago.

What matters most is not where you learned, but what you can do. Build a portfolio. Create real work for real clients—even if they're small businesses or non-profits. Show me results, not certificates.

The Network Effect

One thing university does provide is access to a network. Your classmates become your industry connections. Your professors open doors. The alumni network can be invaluable.

But here's the thing: you can build those networks elsewhere. Attend industry events. Join creative communities online. Contribute to open-source projects. The connections you make matter more than the credentials you hold.

My Verdict

Is it worth going to school for advertising? The honest answer is: it depends. It depends on your learning style, your financial situation, your career goals, and your willingness to learn outside the classroom.

What I can tell you is this: the advertising industry rewards those who can create great work and connect with audiences. How you develop those skills is ultimately up to you.
