How AI is Affecting the Entire Industry in South Africa and the World

By Evans Mathibe • February 2026
Home Back to Articles

In the neon-lit streets of Sandton, where banking towers pierce the Johannesburg skyline, a quiet revolution unfolds. It's not visible in protest marches or parliament debates—it's happening in the algorithms that determine which advertisement you'll see on your phone, which brand will catch your eye, and which message will finally make you click "buy."

Artificial intelligence has arrived in South Africa's advertising industry, and its impact is nothing short of seismic. From the creative agencies of Rosebank to the media buying desks of Cape Town, AI is reshaping every facet of how we conceive, create, and deliver marketing messages.

The Current Landscape

South Africa's advertising industry contributes approximately R45 billion annually to the economy, employing over 30,000 professionals across creative agencies, media shops, and digital consultancies. But here's the uncomfortable truth: many of these roles are now facing fundamental disruption.

"AI isn't going to replace advertising professionals—but advertising professionals who use AI will replace those who don't." — Andrej Karpathy, former Tesla AI Director

The numbers tell a stark story. According to the Interactive Advertising Bureau South Africa (IAB SA), 78% of local agencies have already integrated some form of AI into their operations, up from just 23% in 2022. The acceleration is breathtaking.

But it's not just about adoption rates. It's about the fundamental restructuring of creative work itself. In agencies across Johannesburg, Durban, and Pretoria, copywriters now collaborate with AI tools like ChatGPT and Claude. Designers use Midjourney and DALL-E to rapidly prototype concepts. Media buyers rely on algorithmic bidding systems that optimize spend in real-time.

The Load-Shedding Paradox

Here in South Africa, we face a unique challenge that most of our counterparts in London or San Francisco simply don't understand: load-shedding. When Eskom's grid fails and the lights go out across suburbs from Sandton to Somerset West, our AI systems go with them.

This infrastructure reality has forced South African agencies to become remarkably creative in how they deploy AI. We've learned to build resilient systems, to work offline when necessary, and to appreciate the fundamental role that reliable electricity plays in the digital economy.

According to Statistics South Africa, the country experiences average load-shedding of Stage 2-4 approximately 200 days per year. For advertising agencies, this means:

  • Cloud Dependency: Heavy reliance on cloud-based AI tools becomes a liability when internet connectivity drops
  • Local Processing: Growing interest in edge computing and on-premise solutions that can function during outages
  • Redundancy Planning: Agencies now build failover systems into their creative workflows
  • Hybrid Approaches: Combining cloud AI with local backup systems
"South African agencies have had to solve problems that our peers in developed markets never even consider. This has made us incredibly resourceful." — Sarah Mthembu, Chief Digital Officer at a major Johannesburg agency

The result is a uniquely South African approach to AI adoption—one that's more pragmatic, more resilient, and arguably more sophisticated than our counterparts who have simply moved everything to the cloud.

Creative Destruction or Creative Augmentation?

The doomsayers have had their say. They've predicted mass unemployment in advertising, the death of creativity, and the homogenization of all marketing messages into an AI-generated sludge. But the reality on the ground tells a different story.

78%

of South African agencies have integrated AI into their operations (IAB SA, 2025)

What we're seeing is not the replacement of human creativity, but its amplification. The most successful agencies in South Africa—those winning awards at the Loeries, those securing global accounts—are those that have figured out how to use AI as a force multiplier for human imagination.

"The best creative work still comes from human intuition, emotion, and cultural understanding. But AI helps us get there faster, test more ideas, and refine our craft." — Peter Steinberger, renowned creative director

Consider the workflow of a modern creative team in Johannesburg. Instead of spending hours on initial concepts, they use AI to generate hundreds of rapid prototypes. The human team then selects, refines, and elevates the best ideas—spending their energy on the work that actually requires human judgment: understanding the client's business, connecting with their audience, and crafting messages that resonate on an emotional level.

This shift has profound implications for talent. The skills that matter most are no longer just creative execution—they're creative direction, strategic thinking, and the ability to guide AI tools toward meaningful outputs.

The Global Perspective

While South Africa navigates its unique challenges, the global advertising industry is experiencing its own AI transformation. The numbers are staggering: global spending on AI in advertising is projected to reach $37.8 billion by 2027, according to a report by Statista.

In the United States, tech giants like Google and Meta have integrated AI deeply into their advertising platforms. Performance campaigns now run largely on algorithmic optimization, with human managers acting more as strategic overseers than direct controllers.

European markets, particularly the UK and Germany, have taken a more regulatory approach. The GDPR has forced transparency in how AI uses consumer data, leading to more ethical—and sometimes more limited—applications of machine learning in advertising.

  • United States: $14.2B AI ad spend (2025), dominated by tech platforms
  • United Kingdom: $4.1B, with strong regulatory framework
  • Germany: $3.2B, emphasis on privacy-compliant AI
  • China: $8.7B, rapid adoption with different regulatory approach
  • South Africa: $0.4B, fastest growing market in Africa
"The future of advertising isn't about choosing between human creativity and AI efficiency. It's about finding the perfect synthesis." — Industry analyst report, McKinsey & Company

What makes the South African market unique is our position as the gateway to the continent. With the African Continental Free Trade Area creating new opportunities, South African agencies are increasingly serving as hubs for brands looking to expand across Africa—bringing our AI-powered insights to new markets.

The Road Ahead

As we look to the future, several trends are emerging that will shape the next chapter of AI in advertising. The integration of generative AI with traditional creative workflows will deepen. We'll see more sophisticated personalization at scale, with brands able to create millions of unique ad variations tailored to individual preferences.

But perhaps most importantly, we'll see the rise of what I call "intelligent creativity"—the seamless merger of human insight and machine capability. This isn't about replacing either; it's about creating something entirely new.

"The agencies that will thrive are those that embrace AI not as a tool, but as a collaborator." — Evans Mathibe

For South Africa, the opportunity is immense. We have world-class talent, a sophisticated understanding of both local and continental markets, and an entrepreneurial spirit that's uniquely suited to this moment of transformation.

The question isn't whether AI will change advertising—it's whether we'll lead that change or be swept along by it. At EvansMathibe Agency, we're choosing to lead.
