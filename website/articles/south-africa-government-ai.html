South Africa's Government Response to AI: What Businesses and Creatives Need to Know

By Evans Mathibe Feb 24, 2026 10 min read

As someone who runs a creative agency in South Africa, I've been watching our government's approach to artificial intelligence with keen interest. The decisions being made in Pretoria and Cape Town right now will shape how we do business for years to come. Let me break down what's happening and what it means for you.

The Current Landscape

South Africa hasn't yet enacted comprehensive AI-specific legislation—a reality that puts us behind some of our continental peers but also gives us flexibility to observe and learn from other countries' approaches. What we do have is a collection of existing frameworks that touch on AI-related issues: data protection laws, consumer protection regulations, and sector-specific guidelines.

"South Africa has an opportunity to craft an AI regulatory framework that balances innovation with protection—learning from both the EU's strict approach and the US's more voluntary guidelines."

Key Policy Developments

Data Protection and POPIA

  • The Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA) remains the foundation of our data privacy framework
  • AI systems that process personal data must comply with POPIA requirements
  • The Information Regulator has begun issuing guidance on AI and automated decision-making

National AI Strategy

  • The Department of Communications and Digital Technologies has been developing a national AI strategy
  • Focus areas include skills development, research support, and ethical AI deployment
  • Consultation processes have involved industry stakeholders across sectors

Sector-Specific Approaches

  • Financial services: Reserve Bank guidelines on AI in banking and insurance
  • Healthcare: Discussions on AI in medical diagnosis and patient data
  • Telecommunications: Spectrum and infrastructure considerations for AI systems

What This Means for Creative Businesses

For agencies and creative professionals in South Africa, the current regulatory environment means:

  • Compliance is manageable: As long as you follow existing data protection rules, you're largely covered for now
  • Self-regulation matters: With limited specific AI rules, industry best practices become especially important
  • Opportunity to influence: The government is still shaping its approach, giving businesses a chance to contribute to policy discussions

The Road Ahead

Looking forward, I expect we'll see more specific legislation emerge over the next few years. The direction is likely to include:

  • Greater emphasis on algorithmic transparency and explainability
  • Requirements for bias assessment in AI systems
  • Specific rules for high-stakes AI applications (healthcare, finance, hiring)
  • Consumer protection measures specific to AI-generated content

How to Prepare

My advice for creative businesses is straightforward:

  • Stay informed: Monitor developments from the Department of Communications and the Information Regulator
  • Audit your AI use: Document what AI tools you're using and how they process data
  • Adopt best practices: Even without specific rules, following ethical AI guidelines protects you and your clients
  • Engage with the process: Industry associations are consulted on policy—your voice matters

The Bottom Line

South Africa's approach to AI regulation is still taking shape. For now, creative businesses have considerable freedom to leverage AI tools—but this comes with the responsibility to use them ethically and in compliance with existing laws. The coming years will bring more specific rules, and the agencies that thrive will be those that engage thoughtfully with this transformation rather than waiting to be forced into compliance.

As a creative professional in this country, I see opportunity in this moment. We have the chance to help shape how AI develops in our market—to ensure it serves our people and our businesses rather than simply importing solutions designed for other contexts.
